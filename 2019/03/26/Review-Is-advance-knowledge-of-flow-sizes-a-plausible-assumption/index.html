Review - Is advance knowledge of flow sizes a plausible assumption?

Posted on 2019-03-26 | Edited on 2019-10-12 | In Paper Review

Summary the paper

This work presents FLUX, a framework applying both simple heuristics and machine
learning methods to estimate the flows’ sizes in advance. Evaluations show that
even simple heuristics can achieve high estimation precision and the partial knowledge
aquired by FLUX can benefit a number of flow scheduling for data center networks.

What motivates the work

To better achieve SLAs in data center networks, a number of flow scheduling mechanisms
have been proposed. Among these works, some (e.g. pFabric, PDQ, D3, FastPass, pHost,
Homa, Varys) assume the full knowledge of flow/coflow sizes, while others (e.g. PIAS,
Aalo) schedule flows without such knowledge, claiming to sacrifice certain performance
to benefit and scale deployment.

This work starts by questioning whether flow sizes can be known in advance in practice.
Based on the partial knowledge, authors also study the performance gain in the aspect
of flow scheduling policy. They argue that flow size estimation can guide scheduler
designs with partial knowledge settings and also help measure the efficiency budget.

How FLUX works

After comparing existing solutions (e.g. flow aging, tcp buffer occupancy, system call
monitoring), FLUX chooses to learn flow sizes from past traces. The reason is that
a majority of jobs running in data center network are periodic and thus have predictable
patterns. The challenge is how to get estimation within a short timescale (microsecond).

FLUX gathers a set of campus-scale workloads (Apache Tomcat server, TensorFlow, PageRank,
Spark) as traces. Taking features such as start time, flow gap, CPU, Disk I/O, Memory
I/O, FLUX learns flow size models by several ML models (RNN, GBDT). The authors claim
that these learning results achieve good precision. By applying the knowledge to
scheduler, average mean FCTs can be reduced. This work also mention a counter-intuitive
fact that the knowledge of flow sizes does not always improve performance. That is to
say, sometimes the higher percentage of knowledge predicted, the worse performance.

Strengths

  • In-depth critical analysis on existing work
  • Detailed implementation/tests (self-generated campus traces, full comparisons)

Weaknesses

  • the theoritical part is not coherent
  • feature selection unclear, are the features normalized or not
  • limited use only in periodic job patterns, cannot handle bursts
  • mean FCT is not a good indicator, please check the tail (99th) FCT

Ref: Is advance knowledge of flow sizes a plausible assumption?
